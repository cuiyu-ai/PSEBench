# PSEBench
The code repository of the paper: Benchmarking Proactive Privacy Solicitation and Exploitation from LLMs

## Abstract 
With the growing adoption of personal AI assistants such as OpenClaw, privacy leakage risks from large language model (LLM) agents have become critical. Prior studies show that LLM agents can proactively solicit user private information under system-level adversarial control or indirect prompt injection attacks. The autonomy of personal AI assistants further amplifies this risk. However, existing research remains limited, leaving it unclear when LLMs are more vulnerable, whether they solicit private information in non-attack settings, and how collected private data is exploited. Moreover, it remains unverified whether these privacy risks persist in real-world deployments of LLM agents.

To fill this gap, we investigate three research questions: Do LLMs solicit private information in practical non-attack scenarios, and which domains are affected (RQ1)? Under which contexts are LLMs more likely to solicit private information when exposed to attacks, and what types of information do they prefer (RQ2)? Do LLMs exploit collected private information for harmful purposes (RQ3)? We formalize and systematically evaluate proactive privacy solicitation and exploitation in LLM agents across both attack and non-attack settings. To understand whether such risks arise in real-world deployments, we analyze data from a large-scale agent social platform and identify privacy risks spanning nine domains. Notably, many instances were previously labeled as safe in prior work, suggesting that these risks have been substantially overlooked. Extensive experiments on eight frontier LLMs, including GPT-5.4, and seven datasets show that LLM agents actively solicit private information even in non-attack scenarios. LLMs demonstrate clear preferences for specific types of sensitive data and align requests with task goals. They may even generate deceptive content to elicit private information. Furthermore, LLMs can subsequently exploit the collected information for malicious intent. Our findings reveal a previously underexplored class of privacy risks in LLM agents and provide a foundation for developing more effective defenses.
